Course Outline

This course introduces students to the computational study of complex systems through two complementary lenses. The first part focuses on network science. The second part turns to modeling and simulation, emphasizing how to design complex systems from the bottom up. Students develop agent-based models to simulate social phenomena. Then extend them using large language models (LLMs) to incorporate language-enabled, heterogeneous agents.

Network Science

Analyze complex systems by examining their underlying structures

  • Graph theory fundamentals
  • Network metrics and structural properties
  • Random networks and scale-free networks
  • Community detection algorithms
  • Dynamics on networks

Agent-Based Modeling

Design complex systems from the bottom up

  • Classical agent-based models
  • Epidemic and segregation models
  • Opinion dynamics and cooperation
  • Game-theoretic models
  • Parameter sweeps and sensitivity analysis

LLM-Driven Simulations

Extend models with language-enabled, heterogeneous agents

  • LLM API calls and prompt design
  • LLM-assisted code understanding
  • Language-enabled social simulations
  • Interdisciplinary project design
  • Reproducible computational research

Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, students will be able to:

  1. Analyze real-world complex networks by computing and interpreting core metrics and structural properties.
  2. Explain the dynamics of networks, including the formation and characteristics of random networks and scale-free networks.
  3. Implement and evaluate dynamics on networks through classical agent-based models, explain emergent patterns from individual incentives, and conduct parameter sweeps and sensitivity analyses.
  4. Formulate real-world social questions as clear model specifications (agents, rules, data inputs) and justify modeling choices and assumptions. Compare and apply different approaches to building agent-based models, including rule-based methods, game-theoretic models, and LLM-driven approaches.
  5. Design and execute interdisciplinary research projects in groups by selecting themes, conducting literature reviews, identifying research problems, and developing reproducible code and analyses with tools taught in class.

Class Participation (20%)

Self-introductions (10%)

Brief self-introductions help establish connections with peers and contribute to a more interactive class atmosphere. This also offers practice for introducing yourself in professional settings.

Project Discussions (10%)

Participate in discussions related to the class project, sharing ideas, challenges, and progress. This is an opportunity to receive feedback from peers and the instructor.

Lab Engagement (20%)

Actively participate in in-class lab activities and tasks where you will gain hands-on experience in designing and analyzing complex systems.

Midterm Project (30%)

Explore a research stream that interests you. Select a paper from the required reading list, thoroughly read it, explore papers that cite it, and find a research stream to write a literature review about. Submit a written report summarizing the literature review, including critiques and proposed research questions. Give a 15-minute presentation (5 min Q&A) summarizing expected contributions and plans of action. Students may work individually or in teams of two.

Final Project (30%)

Pick up your proposal from the midterm project and make progress following your previous plan of action. Submit a final report documenting your research process, findings, and conclusions. Give a final in-class presentation explaining your research question, methods, results, and contributions. For team projects, each member must summarize their contribution, which will also be used for grade evaluation.

The course employs a mix of teaching strategies and activities to encourage active learning, critical thinking, and hands-on experience. These activities are designed to foster collaboration, creativity, and a deep understanding of the key concepts in complex systems.

Late Policy

Under normal circumstances, coursework assignments will be marked within two weeks. If an assignment is submitted after the due date without prior permission from the instructor, it will receive a grade of zero. The above grade descriptor is subject to change.

Academic Honesty

Attention is drawn to University policy and regulations on honesty in academic work, and to the disciplinary guidelines and procedures applicable to breaches of such policy and regulations. Details may be found at https://registry.cuhk.edu.cn/en/page/30.

With each assignment, students will be required to submit a signed declaration that they are aware of these policies, regulations, guidelines and procedures. For group projects, all students of the same group should be asked to sign on the declaration. Only the final version of the assignment should be submitted via VeriGuide.
